You know the look. The clenched shoulders, the "I don't want to" that isn't really
about the thing they're refusing, the meltdown that's three sizes too big for
whatever set it off. When your kid is wound up or worried, the fastest-looking fix
is right there in your pocket: hand over the tablet, let the noise and color take
over, and buy everyone a few quiet minutes. And it does go quiet. For a while.

But here's the catch. **A screen is very good at grabbing a kid's attention. It is
not very good at settling one.** The worry doesn't actually go anywhere. It just
waits underneath the bright, fast content, and when the screen switches off it is
often right back, sometimes bigger. What a keyed-up kid usually needs isn't more
input. It's less. Something slow, something they can feel in their hands and their
feet, something that gently pulls them back into the actual room they are standing in.

## Start with the senses

When a kid is spun up, their mind is usually racing somewhere other than the
present, off in the thing they are dreading or the thought they cannot shake. The
quickest way back is through the senses. **A mind that is busy noticing has less
room to spin.** And it is genuinely hard to smell a flower and worry about tomorrow
at the very same moment.

You don't need anything fancy. Try naming things out loud together: three things you
can hear right now, two things you can see, one thing you can feel under your hands.
Go slow. Let the answers be silly. The point isn't to get it right, it's to give a
busy brain one small, real thing to hold instead of the big swirl.

Smell is a sneaky-good anchor. Head outside and go on [The Smell Hunt](/app/quest/the-smell-hunt):
cut grass, a neighbor's dinner, rain on warm pavement, the inside of a flower.
Naming what you smell pulls a kid straight out of their head and into right now,
which is exactly where a worried mind needs to land.

## Slow everything all the way down

A wound-up body wants to speed up, so your job is to model the opposite, quietly.
Lower your voice, slow your own movements, and let the pace of the moment drop. Kids
borrow their calm from us more than we realize, so a grown-up who slows down gives a
child quiet permission to do the same.

One of the simplest ways to slow a racing afternoon is to go outside and look up.
Lie back on the grass together and play [Cloud Zoo](/app/quest/cloud-zoo): find the
dog, the dragon, the slow scoop of ice cream drifting past. There is something about
lying under a big open sky that lets a tight little chest unclench. No talking
required, no problem to solve, just two people watching clouds do nothing in
particular. That is the whole point.

## Give their hands something to do

Sometimes the fastest route to a calmer kid isn't talking about the feeling at all.
It is sitting shoulder to shoulder and doing something small and repetitive with
your hands. The busywork soaks up the jittery energy, and the side-by-side setup,
with no eye contact and no pressure, is often exactly when kids start to talk, if
they want to.

Painting is perfect for this. Gather a few smooth stones and set up a [Rock Painting Gallery](/app/quest/rock-painting-gallery)
on the back step: one rock at a time, whatever colors they reach for, no rules about
how it should look. The rhythm of it, dip and paint and dry and admire, is calming
all on its own. If they want to talk while they paint, wonderful. If they would
rather just paint, that is wonderful too.
