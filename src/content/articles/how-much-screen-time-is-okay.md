It's probably the most-Googled parenting question there is: *how much screen time is
actually okay?* And most answers either make you feel guilty or give you a number so
strict it collapses the first busy week. So let's do this honestly — real
guidelines, why they're not the whole story, and permission to stop stressing.

First, the big truth: **there is no perfect number, and a screen is not the enemy.**
What matters far more than counting minutes is *what* the screen replaces and *how*
it's used. A rushed hour of a tablet so you can cook dinner in peace is not going to
undo your child. Relax on that front — you're doing fine.

## The rough guidelines by age

Experts (like the American Academy of Pediatrics) give ranges, not rules. Think of
these as a loose compass, not a stopwatch — and always trust your pediatrician over
a blog:

- **Under 18 months:** aside from video calls with family, screens are best kept to
  a minimum. This is the age where real-world back-and-forth matters most.
- **18 months to 2 years:** a little is okay if you're watching *together* and
  talking about it. Co-viewing turns passive watching into learning.
- **Ages 2 to 5:** a common guideline is around **1 hour a day** of good-quality
  stuff on weekdays — but "roughly an hour, most days" is a much healthier goal than
  an exact timer.
- **Ages 6 and up:** there's no magic number here. The advice shifts to keeping
  screens from crowding out the things that matter: **sleep, moving their body,
  meals, and real-world play.** If those are protected, the leftover screen time is
  rarely the problem.

## Why quality beats the clock

Two hours are not all the same. Building something in a creative app, video-calling
grandma, or watching a documentary together is worlds apart from two hours of
autoplay. A few things that matter more than the total:

- **What it replaces.** Screen time *instead of* sleep or outdoor play is the real
  cost. Screen time during a long car ride costs you almost nothing.
- **Watching with them, when you can.** Co-viewing and asking "what do you think
  happens next?" turns screen time into together-time.
- **Content over quantity.** Slow, calm, creative content beats fast, loud,
  designed-to-be-endless content — even for the same number of minutes.

## The shift that actually helps

Here's the mindset that takes the pressure off: **stop trying to subtract screens,
and start trying to add everything else.** When the day is full of things a kid
genuinely wants to do — outside, hands-on, with you — screens naturally take up less
room. You're not fighting the tablet; you're just making the rest of life more
appealing than it.

That's a much kinder game to play than watching a timer, and it's the one you can
actually win.
