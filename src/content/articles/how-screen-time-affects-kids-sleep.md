Screen time hurts your kid's sleep in two clear ways: the bright light delays melatonin, the
hormone that makes us drowsy, and the stimulating content (a game, a fast video, a cliffhanger)
revs the mind up right when it needs to power down. The fix is one of the most reliable in
parenting: no screens for at least an hour before bed, keep devices out of the bedroom, and
put a calm routine in their place. Do that and you'll likely see easier nights within a week.

If your kid fights sleep, lies awake, or wakes up cranky, the tablet before bed is worth a hard
look. The good news is this is one of the most fixable sleep problems there is. You don't need a
sleep clinic. Here's what's actually going on and what to do about it.

## What screens do to sleep

Two things, mainly. First, the **bright light** from a screen tells the brain it's still
daytime, which delays melatonin, the hormone that makes us sleepy. So the body doesn't get the
"time to wind down" signal on schedule. Second, the **content itself** is stimulating: a game,
a fast video, a cliffhanger, all of it revs the mind up right when it needs to power down.

Put together, a kid who's on a screen until lights-out is being told "stay alert" by both the
light and the show, and then asked to fall asleep immediately. No wonder it's a battle.

## Protect the hour before bed

The single highest-value change: **no screens for at least an hour before bed.** That hour lets
melatonin do its job and lets a busy mind actually settle. It's the one rule that fixes the most
with the least effort.

## Get the devices out of the bedroom

Screens in the bedroom are too tempting, the late scroll, the "just one more" under the covers.
Where possible, charge phones and tablets *outside* the bedroom overnight. Out of reach is far
easier than relying on willpower, especially for older kids.

## Replace the wind-down

Taking the screen away works far better when something calm takes its place:

- **A book or a story.** The classic wind-down for a reason.
- **Quiet, low-stakes play** in the last stretch, nothing thrilling.
- **A predictable routine** that ends the same way every night, so the body learns the pattern.

Screens hurt sleep in two clear ways: the light delays sleepiness and the content winds the mind
up. The fix is refreshingly simple: keep screens out of the hour before bed and out of the
bedroom, and put a calm routine in their place. Do that and you'll likely see easier nights and
