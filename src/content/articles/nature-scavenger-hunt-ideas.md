A good nature scavenger hunt is a short list of things for kids to find outside, six to
ten items long, matched to where you are. In the backyard, hunt for a smooth rock, a
feather, something red, and a stick shaped like a letter. At the park, add an acorn, a
bug, and bark you can rub. At the beach, look for a shell, sea glass, and a crab track.
Give them a bag to collect in and a rule to spot living things without grabbing them.

It works because it takes a vague, easy-to-refuse "go play in the yard" and turns it
into a mission with a finish line. It costs nothing and needs almost no prep. Here's how
to run one, plus ready-made lists for wherever you happen to be.

## How to run one

- **Keep the list short.** Six to ten items is the sweet spot. Too few ends fast, too
  many gets abandoned halfway.
- **Give them a bag or bucket.** Collecting is half the fun. For things they can't or
  shouldn't take, a checklist and a pencil to tick off works just as well.
- **Make one rule for living things.** "Spot it, don't grab it" for bugs and animals
  keeps everyone kind, and a photo on your phone still counts as a catch.
- **Add a silly wildcard.** "Something that looks like a face" or "the coolest stick"
  makes them look closely instead of grabbing the first obvious thing.

## Backyard hunt

Perfect for a quick after-dinner mission:

- something smooth and something rough
- three different kinds of leaf
- a rock smaller than your thumbnail
- something an insect might live under
- a flower, or anything yellow
- a sound you can hear but not see

## Park hunt

A step up in variety for a bigger space:

- a feather
- an acorn, a seed, or a pinecone
- bark with a pattern in it
- a Y-shaped stick
- something that traveled on the wind to get here
- a tree older than you (take your best guess)

## Beach hunt

For sand-and-water days:

- a shell with a spiral
- a piece of sea glass or a smooth pebble
- a bird footprint in the sand
- something the tide left behind
- a crab, or a shell a crab could live in
- a rock with a stripe running through it

## Match it to their age

Little kids do best with color and texture, so lean on "something soft" and "something
blue." Older kids like a puzzle in it, so "something older than you" and "evidence an
animal was here" land better. Mixed ages? Give everyone the same list and let the big
kids help the little ones. It quietly turns a race into a team.
