There is a specific face a kid makes the first time they mix two ordinary things
and something happens. Eyebrows up, mouth open, a tiny gasp. You cannot get that
face from a video. A kid watching a science channel is a spectator, politely
impressed and then on to the next thing. A kid who just made a cup fizz over onto
the kitchen counter is something else: a scientist who is now fairly sure they have
powers.

That is the whole difference, and it is worth protecting. **A screen can show your
kid that something is cool. A real experiment lets them find it out for
themselves.** None of this needs a kit or a lab, either. Most of the best
experiments are already hiding in your kitchen.

## Start with the kitchen classics

The baking soda volcano earns its reputation. Put a couple of spoonfuls of baking
soda in a cup, add a squirt of dish soap and a drop of food coloring, then pour in
vinegar and step back. The foamy eruption is carbon dioxide gas, a real reaction
you can run again and again. Set the cup in a mound of dirt outside and it becomes a
proper volcano.

While you are in the kitchen, try dancing raisins. Drop a few into a clear glass of
fizzy soda water and watch them ride the bubbles up, tip over, and sink back down on
a loop. It is oddly hypnotic, and it is buoyancy in action.

Here is the one move that turns a fun mess into actual science: **resist the urge to
explain.** When your kid asks why the raisins bob, ask it back. "Huh, why do you
think?" A guess they reasoned out beats a fact you handed them, every time.

## Play with water

Water might be the most patient science teacher in the house. Start with sink or
float: fill a big bowl, gather ten small objects, and have your kid predict yes or
no before each one goes in. That prediction is not a warm-up to the experiment. It
is the experiment.

For something slower and stranger, set up a walking-water rainbow. Line up a few
glasses, fill every other one with water tinted by food coloring, then bridge the
gaps with folded paper towels. Over an hour or two the color climbs the towels and
fills the empty glasses on its own. Nobody touches it. It just walks.

## Feel the invisible forces

Some of the best experiments involve forces you cannot see but can absolutely prove.
Rub a balloon on someone's hair for ten seconds, then hold it near a thin stream of
tap water and watch the water bend toward it. That pull is static electricity, and
it will also stick the balloon to a wall and lift little scraps of torn paper.

Sound is another invisible you can chase. Stretch a rubber band between two fingers
and pluck it, then pull it tighter and pluck again to hear the pitch climb. Then
take the idea outdoors with [The Echo Test](/app/quest/the-echo-test): find a big
wall or an empty parking structure, send out a good loud shout, and hunt for the
exact spot where the world shouts back. Sound bouncing off a hard surface is the
whole trick behind an echo.

## Take the lab outside

The biggest experiment of all runs for free right over your head. Weather is science
you can watch change by the hour. When the sky turns dramatic, let your kid become a
[Storm Watcher](/app/quest/storm-watcher) from a safe, dry window: count the slow
seconds between a flash of lightning and its rumble of thunder. Because light
reaches you almost instantly and sound lags behind, a longer gap means the storm is
farther away. No standing out in the rain required.

For the patient scientist, wrap a dried bean in a damp paper towel, tuck it into a
clear jar on the windowsill, and keep the towel moist. Over several days a root
curls out, then a pale shoot reaches up. It is the slowest experiment here and
somehow the most thrilling, because your kid grew it.

## The takeaway

You do not have to be good at science to raise a kid who loves it. You just have to
let the fizz happen, ask "why do you think?", and stay out of the way while they
work it out. Pick one experiment and try it this afternoon, or
