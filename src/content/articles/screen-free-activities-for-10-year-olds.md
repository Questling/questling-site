You suggest an activity, and your 10-year-old gives you the look. Not the tantrum look from a few years back, the newer one. The slow blink that says "I am not five." Craft kits feel babyish. A nature scavenger hunt with cartoon animals on it? Please. Somewhere in the last year the stuff that used to work started landing wrong.

The thing is, they still want to be busy, and they still want to be off the screen more than they admit. What changed is the bar. At ten, kids want to feel a little bit trusted, a little bit capable, and a little bit like they are getting away with something grown-up. Get that part right and screen-free stops being a fight.

## Give them a real skill, not a craft

Ten-year-olds are past pipe cleaners. They want to make things that actually work. Cooking is the easiest win here, because the output is real and everyone eats it.

- **Hand over one full meal.** Not "help stir," but "you are in charge of dinner Thursday." Let them pick it, find the recipe, and cook it while you stay nearby but quiet. The independence is the whole point.
- **Teach one grown-up skill on purpose.** Using a real knife, lighting the stove, sewing a button back on, checking tire pressure on a bike. These feel like being let into the adult world, which is exactly what a ten-year-old is hungry for.
- **Let the first try be bad.** Burned rice teaches more than a perfect result you rescued. Resist the urge to take over.

## Let them go somewhere on their own

At ten, the world getting a little bigger is the reward. If your neighborhood allows it, a solo or with-a-friend errand is huge.

Biking to a friend's house, walking to the corner store with a short list and real cash, or riding to the park and being back by a set time. You are not just filling an afternoon, you are handing over a small piece of trust, and they feel it. Start with a route you both know, agree on a check-in, and let them own it. The point is not the errand. The point is that nobody was hovering.

## Build something that takes more than an afternoon

Bigger kids can hold a project in their head for days, and that is a skill worth feeding. Give them something with a little scope.

- **A fort or a den that gets better over a week.** Pallets, cardboard, rope, whatever is around. They plan, they revise, they problem-solve.
- **A working contraption.** A marble run down the stairs, a pulley to lift a bucket up to a treehouse, a cardboard machine with actual moving parts.
- **A patch of garden that is theirs.** Their seeds, their watering, their harvest. The wait is the lesson.

The magic ingredient is that you do not manage it. You supply materials and then get out of the way.

## Make it a challenge, not an activity

Reframe the exact same task as a dare and a ten-year-old leans in. The words matter.

Instead of "go outside," try "can you learn to do an ollie by the end of the week." Instead of "read a book," try "beat your own record for how many chapters before lights out." Skateboarding, learning to juggle, teaching yourself a card trick, timing how fast you can build and knock down a domino chain. The framing turns a chore into something they chose to conquer.

## Let them be the boss of you

Once in a while, flip the roles. Let your kid run the show and watch how seriously they take it.

They plan the family walk and pick the route. They design an obstacle course in the backyard and make you do it. They cook while you are the assistant who is only allowed to do what you are told. Being trusted to lead, and being right, is a feeling ten-year-olds do not get often enough. It is also quietly teaching planning, sequencing, and how it feels when other people rely on you.
