There's a special kind of chaos that happens the moment you turn the screens off and two kids
are left to entertain each other. Within ninety seconds someone's touching someone, someone's
"not sharing," and you're refereeing a conflict over a toy nobody wanted five minutes ago. It's
enough to make you hand the tablets right back just for the quiet.

But siblings without screens don't have to mean siblings at war. A lot of the fighting comes
from boredom plus competition. Give them something to do *together*, aimed at a shared goal
instead of against each other, and the same two kids who were bickering will suddenly be a team.

## Make it cooperative, not competitive

Competition between siblings is lighter fluid. "Who can do it best" ends in tears; "let's build
this together" ends in a fort. So steer toward activities where they're on the same side.

- **Build something as a team.** A blanket fort, a marble run, a giant tower, a pretend shop.
- **Give them one shared mission,** not two separate turns to fight over.
- **Skip the "winner."** If an activity has to have one, expect it to end badly.

## Give them a project bigger than both of them

Siblings unite around a good project. A treasure hunt through the house. A play they perform for
you after dinner. A "restaurant" they run together, one cooks, one serves. An obstacle course in
the backyard they design and then both attempt. The bigger and more absorbing the mission, the
less energy is left for squabbling.

## Work with the age gap, not against it

If there's a big gap, don't force them to play as equals, that's where it breaks down. Give the
older one a *role*: the guide, the teacher, the team captain. Most big kids love being trusted
with a little responsibility, and most little kids love the attention. Suddenly the gap is the
point, not the problem.

## Let them be bored together

You don't have to fix every dull moment. Some of the best sibling play, the invented games, the
secret worlds, the inside jokes, is born in the boredom right after you *stop* entertaining them.
If you rescue every quiet stretch, you skip the part where they figure each other out. A little
boredom, shared, does real work.

Siblings without screens will never be perfectly peaceful, and that's normal. But aim them at a
shared goal instead of at each other, give the older one a role, and let a little boredom do its
work, and you'll get a lot more "we built this" and a lot less "he started it."
