Little kids will chase a bubble across the yard. Tweens will look at you like you've lost
your mind. Somewhere around ten, "go play outside" stops working, the toys get labeled
babyish, and the phone or the console becomes the center of gravity. It's easy to feel like
you've lost the kid who used to build forts, and easy to just give up and hand over the
screen.

You haven't lost them. A ten-to-thirteen-year-old isn't too old for real-world stuff, they're
too old for stuff that feels like it's *for* little kids. The trick is to stop offering
activities and start offering independence, challenge, and things that feel a bit grown-up.

## Give them real independence, not busywork

Tweens are wired to pull away and do things on their own. A craft you set up for them reads
as babyish. The exact same energy, aimed at something real, lands completely differently.

- **Let them go somewhere alone-ish.** Bike to a friend's, walk to the shop, take the dog
  around the block. The freedom is the appeal.
- **Give them a real job with real stakes.** Cook one family dinner start to finish. Build
  or fix something that actually gets used.
- **Trust them with something bigger than they expect.** Being trusted is intoxicating at
  this age, and it beats any activity you could plan.

## Tap into what they already like

The fastest way to lose a tween is to ignore what they're into. The fastest way to win is
to take it seriously and push it into the real world.

Into gaming? There's a whole physical world around it: build a real thing they've built in
a game, learn to actually code a tiny one, get into strategy board games. Into videos about
something? Let them *do* that thing, cooking, art, skating, makeup, whatever it is. You're
not fighting their interest. You're giving it somewhere to go that isn't a screen.

## Let them earn something that matters

"Because I said so" is dead by now, and a sticker chart is insulting to a twelve-year-old.
But tweens will absolutely work toward something they actually want, if the reward is real
and the deal is respectful.

Later bedtime on the weekend. A say in the plans. Money toward a thing they're saving for.
Tie effort to something they genuinely care about and treat it like a fair trade between
near-equals, not a bribe for a toddler, and you'll get buy-in you didn't expect.

## Make it social

At this age, the friends *are* the activity. A tween who groans at a family walk will
happily spend three hours outside if a friend is there. So build that in.

Invite the friend along. Make your house the one where kids come to do stuff. A lot of what
looks like "my tween only wants screens" is really "my tween wants to be with their people,"
and screens are just where their people currently are. Move the people into the real world
and the tween usually follows.

counts as worth doing. Trade busywork for independence, meet them where their interests
already are, make the reward real, and bring their friends in. Do that and the kid who
"only wants screens" turns out to still be in there, just waiting for an offer that doesn't
feel like it's for a baby.
