Working from home with a kid around is its own special challenge. You've got a call in
ten minutes, an inbox that won't quit, and a small person who wants you *right now*. The
tablet is right there, and it works, so it becomes the default. No judgment, that's just
what happens when you're trying to do two full-time jobs in the same room.

But screens all day, every workday adds up fast, and you can feel it in how the evenings
go. So the goal here isn't to never use one. It's to have a few reliable, screen-free
things you can set your kid up with that actually buy you a real stretch of focus, so the
tablet stops being the only tool in the box.

## Set up "invitations to play" before you start

The trick that works best is doing five minutes of prep before your work block, not
during it. Kids will happily dive into something that's already set out and ready, but
"go find something to do" usually ends with them back at your elbow.

- **Lay it out like a little scene.** Animals arranged mid-adventure, blocks stacked into
  the start of a tower, crayons and paper already open. A ready scene pulls them in.
- **Rotate the toys.** Put half the toys away for a couple of weeks, then swap. The "old"
  ones feel brand new again, which buys you far more focused time.
- **Keep a special box for work time only.** Toys that only come out when you're on a call
  feel like a treat, so they hold attention longer.

## Match the activity to the length of the task

Not every work block is the same, so don't reach for the same setup every time. A quick
email needs something different than a two-hour stretch.

- **Ten minutes:** a quick puzzle, a bit of playdough, a sticker book.
- **Half an hour:** an "invitation to play" scene, building something, a big drawing
  project with a theme ("draw our whole street").
- **The long haul:** a proper mission. Something with a goal and a few steps that they can
  work through on their own while you're heads-down.

## Give them a job, not just a distraction

Kids concentrate longest on things that feel real and important. "Here, be quiet" doesn't
land. "I need your help with something" does.

Give them a genuine task: sort the recycling, build the tallest tower they can and photograph
it, set the table, water the plants, tidy one specific shelf. A job with a clear finish
makes them feel useful instead of shooed away, and useful buys you a lot more time than
bored.

## Plan your hardest block around their best time

You know your kid's rhythm. Most have a stretch in the day when they play happily on their
own and a stretch when they melt down and need you. Line your most important, can't-be-
interrupted work up with their good window, and save the low-focus tasks (the ones you can
do with a kid on your lap) for the hard hours. You can't out-discipline a tired toddler, so
work *with* the day instead of against it.

## Where Questling fits

For those longer blocks when you genuinely need forty uninterrupted minutes, a single quest
does more than a pile of toys, and that's exactly what [Questling](/) is for. It lives on
your phone and hands your kid a real, screen-free mission with a few steps to work through
(a backyard hunt, something to build, a challenge to complete), so they're happily busy with
a goal while you're on your call. They do it, earn gems toward rewards you set, and come back
proud instead of restless.

Working from home with kids will never be tidy, and some days the screen wins. That's fine.
But with a few things set up and ready, a job that makes them feel useful, and your hard work
lined up with their good hours, you can get real work done and keep the tablet as a backup
instead of the whole plan.
