Bedtime with a screen is a trap that feels like a solution. The tablet keeps them quiet while
you tidy up, but it also winds them up right when you need them winding down. Then you try to
take it away and get a second wind of protest, a kid who's now both tired and amped, and a
lights-out that drags on forever.

Here's the thing screens do at bedtime: the light tells their brain it's daytime, and the
content keeps their mind racing. It's the opposite of what sleep needs. So the most useful
bedtime rule might just be "screens off well before bed," and then a calm, boring, predictable
routine to carry them down.

## Make the wind-down the same every night

Kids fall asleep faster when their body knows what's coming. A fixed order acts like a runway:
each step signals the next, and sleep arrives at the end without a fight.

Pick a sequence and keep it identical: bath, pajamas, teeth, a story, lights out. The exact
steps matter less than the *sameness*. When it never changes, their body starts powering down
on autopilot before they even reach the bed.

## Swap the screen for something calm

You're not just removing the tablet, you're filling the gap with something that actually helps
them settle.

- **Books, always.** A story is the gold standard wind-down, and it's time together too.
- **Quiet play or drawing** in the last stretch, nothing exciting or competitive.
- **A little chat.** "Best thing about today?" beats one more video for helping a busy mind unclench.

## Keep the last hour boring on purpose

The hour before bed should be the dullest hour of the day. No screens, no roughhousing, no
sugar, no thrilling new game. Dim the lights, lower your voice, slow everything down. Boring is
the goal here, not a failure. A calm, uneventful hour is what lets a kid actually drop off.

## Set a clear "screens off" line

You don't have to ban screens all evening, you just have to move them out of the danger zone.
The cleanest rule is a firm one everyone knows: screens off at least an hour before bed, no
exceptions, no "just one more." Once it's simply the rule and not a nightly negotiation, the
arguing mostly fades, because there's nothing left to argue about.

A smooth bedtime isn't about a perfect chart. It's a fixed wind-down order, a calm swap for the
screen, a deliberately boring last hour, and a clear line on when screens stop. Set that up and
lights-out slowly stops being the worst part of the day.
