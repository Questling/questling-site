Some kids can happily stare out a car window for an hour. Most cannot. Somewhere
around the twenty-minute mark the wiggling starts, then the "are we there yet," and
the easiest fix in the world is sitting right there in the cup holder: a tablet. If
that's your current car strategy, no judgment. A screen buys quiet, and quiet is
precious when you're the one driving.

But screens in the car have a way of becoming the *only* thing that works, and then
every drive turns into a negotiation. The good news is that car time is secretly great
for the kind of play a screen crushes the rest of the day: talking, noticing,
imagining, being a little bored together. Here are the things that genuinely hold up,
minus the guilt trip.

## The classic games still work

There's a reason these have survived generations of road trips. They need zero gear,
they stretch to almost any age, and they turn the boring view out the window into the
whole point.

- **I Spy.** The old standby, still undefeated for little kids. In a moving car, use
  colors instead of objects ("I spy something red") so the answer doesn't vanish
  before they can guess it.
- **The license-plate hunt.** Call out plates from different states and keep a running
  list, or just hunt for the letters of your kid's name in order. Older kids can try
  to spot the numbers 0 through 9.
- **Twenty questions.** Perfect for kids who are past I Spy. One person thinks of a
  thing, everyone else gets twenty yes-or-no questions to guess it. It's quietly
  teaching deduction, and it eats up miles.
- **The color game.** Pick a color at the start of the drive and count how many cars
  you see in it. First to ten wins. Dead simple, weirdly addictive.

## Let someone else tell the story

When you're out of talking energy, and you will be, audio is the screen-free secret
weapon. It keeps your kid's eyes off a screen and their imagination switched on, which
is the whole difference.

- **Audiobooks.** A good series can turn a dreaded long drive into "can we sit in the
  driveway until the chapter ends." Your library almost certainly lends free
  audiobooks through an app, so you don't have to buy a thing.
- **Story podcasts.** There are wonderful kids' story and mystery podcasts made exactly
  for this, in tidy fifteen to thirty minute episodes. Queue a few before you leave so
  you're not fumbling with your phone at a red light.
- **Sing-along playlists.** Never underestimate a loud family car singalong. It's
  silly, it burns energy, and nobody is staring at a device.

## A few quiet things for busy hands

Some kids need something to *do*, not just something to hear. The trick is picking
things that won't create a disaster you'll be cleaning out of the seats for a month.

- **Sticker books and reusable cling sets.** Low mess, endlessly re-doable, and they
  buy a solid chunk of quiet.
- **Magnetic travel games.** Tic-tac-toe, checkers, and little magnetic mazes stay put
  on a tray and don't go rolling under the seat at every turn.
- **Water-reveal coloring books.** You "paint" with a water pen, the color appears,
  then it dries and resets. No markers, no stains, basically magic.
- **A small activity pouch.** Keep a zippered bag in the car with a few of these, and
  rotate what's inside now and then so it feels fresh. Out of sight between trips means
  it's still interesting on the next one.

## Make the drive itself the game

The best car entertainment is often the drive itself, reframed. Kids will happily do a
thing that feels like a mission when the exact same thing framed as "just look outside"
gets an eye-roll.

- Give them a **spotting mission**: find five red barns, three dogs, a horse, and a
  water tower before you arrive.
- Make them the **navigator**: "tell me when you see the big bridge" gives a little kid
  an important job and a reason to watch the road.
- Try a **story chain**: someone starts a story with one sentence, the next person adds
  a sentence, and it gets gloriously ridiculous by mile ten.
- Hand over a **countdown**: "we're watching for exit 24, help me catch the signs"
  turns restlessness into a shared goal.

## Where Questling fits

A car ride is one long pocket of "what do we do now," which is exactly the gap
[Questling](/) is built for. It lives on your phone, not your kid's, and it can turn
the drive into a quick quest: a spotting challenge, a round of I Spy, a story to invent
on the way. Your kid does it in the real world, earns gems toward rewards you choose,
and the trip becomes something you did together instead of something everyone survived
on separate screens.

None of this has to be perfect. Some drives you'll play twenty questions until you're
begging for mercy, and some days you'll cave and hand over the tablet, and that's fine
too. But a glovebox with a couple of these tricks in it means the screen stops being
the only button you can press. Most of the time, a good game and a story beat a tablet
anyway. Kids just need someone to start it.
