The idea of a screen-free family night sounds lovely right up until you announce it and
get a room full of groans. Everyone had their own plan, the TV was already on, and now
you're the person who ruined the evening. So it quietly dies after one attempt, and the
screens win by default.

The fix isn't more willpower. It's lowering the stakes so far that the night can't fail.
A good family night is not a wholesome event you host. It's a small, predictable habit
that's easier to say yes to than to fight. Here's how to make one that sticks.

## Pick a time and protect it

The single biggest reason family night fails is that it's vague. "We should do this more"
never survives a busy week. Instead, pin it down: same night, same rough time, every week.

Friday after dinner. Sunday before bath. It doesn't matter which, it matters that it's
the *same* one, so nobody has to decide or negotiate. Once it's just "what we do on
Fridays," the arguing mostly disappears, because there's nothing left to argue about.

## Keep the bar embarrassingly low

Family night does not need to be special. The pressure to make it magical is exactly
what makes you skip it. Aim for "easy and repeatable," not "memorable."

- **One activity is plenty.** A single board game, a puzzle, a deck of cards. You are
  not running a variety show.
- **Snacks do a lot of heavy lifting.** Popcorn or a bit of dessert turns an ordinary
  night into an event for almost no effort.
- **Forty minutes counts.** You don't need to fill the whole evening. A short, good
  night beats a long, forced one.

## Let the kids run it

Kids protect what they help build. If family night is something being done *to* them,
they'll resist it. If it's partly theirs, they'll defend it.

Let them pick the game, or take turns choosing each week. Give one kid the job of
setting up, another the job of picking the snack. The more ownership they have, the more
it becomes a thing they look forward to instead of a thing they endure.

## Have a few reliable go-tos

Decision fatigue kills family night. If every week starts with "so what should we do,"
you'll bail. Keep a short, dependable list on the fridge so the answer is already there:

- **A stack of board games** everyone actually likes, not the ones that end in tears.
- **A card game or two** for the nights nobody has the energy for setup.
- **A build-something night**, a bake-something night, a story-you-make-up-together
  night for variety.

Rotate through the same handful. Familiar is a feature here, not a bug.

You don't need a perfect weekly ritual with matching pajamas. You need a protected slot,
a low bar, a bit of kid buy-in, and a short list of go-tos. Do that a few weeks in a row
and family night stops being a thing you push for and starts being a thing they remind
*you* about.
