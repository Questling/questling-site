The screen-free restaurant activities that actually keep kids happy fall into three
buckets: drawing and paper games on the placemat (the squiggle game, guess-my-drawing,
tic-tac-toe), a tiny bag of quiet toys and crayons you pack ahead, and fun
would-you-rather questions that turn the wait into real conversation. Together those
three cover the twenty minutes between ordering and food that usually sends a tablet
sliding across the table.

No shame in it. Eating out with little kids is genuinely hard, and you deserve to
taste your own meal while it's warm. But a screen at dinner also means your kid checks
out of the table entirely, and the whole reason you went out together quietly
disappears. The good news is that a handful of low-effort tricks keep them at the
table and in the moment, no charging cable required.

## Pack a tiny table bag

Half the battle is just having something on hand. Keep a small zip pouch in your bag
or the car with a few restaurant-only bits, and it stays interesting precisely because
it only comes out here.

- **A few crayons or twist-up colored pencils.** Most kid-friendly places hand out
  paper, and for the ones that don't, a little pad lives in the bag.
- **A couple of small, quiet toys.** A few figurines, a wind-up car, a tiny sticker
  book. Nothing with a hundred pieces to lose under the table.
- **Pipe cleaners or bendy craft sticks.** Silent, endlessly re-shapeable, and they
  can be twisted into rings, animals, and glasses right there at the table.

Rotate what's inside every so often so it feels new. Out of sight between dinners is
what keeps it worth pulling out.

## Games that need nothing but the table

When the bag runs dry, the table itself is full of games:

- **The squiggle game.** You draw a random squiggle, they turn it into something. Then
  they squiggle and you finish it. Weirdly great at every age.
- **Guess my drawing.** Slow-motion Pictionary on the back of the placemat. Whoever
  guesses gets to draw next.
- **The quiet game, used sparingly.** "Who can stay quiet until the waiter comes back"
  is a classic for a reason. Don't lean on it every time, but it has saved many a meal.
- **Tic-tac-toe and dots-and-boxes.** All you need is the paper placemat and one pen
  passed between you.

## Try an actual conversation

Restaurants are one of the rare times you're all sitting still and facing each other,
so it's a small gift for real talking. Skip "how was your day," which earns you a
one-word "fine," and try something with a door in it:

- "Would you rather be able to fly or turn invisible?"
- "What was the best part of today, and the most boring part?"
- "If you owned this restaurant, what would be on the menu?"
- "Make up a story about the people at that table over there."

Kids will happily talk your ear off when the question is actually fun to answer.

## Where Questling fits

That wait for the food is a perfect little pocket of nothing, which is exactly what
[Questling](/) is good at filling. It lives on your phone, not your kid's, and it can
turn the wait into a quick quest: a drawing challenge, a spot-something-around-the-room
game, a story to invent together. Your kid does it right there at the table, earns gems
toward rewards you choose, and dinner stays something you shared instead of something
everyone scrolled through.

You don't have to run a full activity program at every meal. One drawing game and one
good question usually gets you from ordering to dessert. The tablet can stay in the
bag, and you might just get to finish a hot meal and a real conversation.
