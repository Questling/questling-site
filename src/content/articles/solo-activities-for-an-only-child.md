The best solo activities for an only child are self-directed missions with a clear
goal, a little challenge, and total ownership: building projects, backyard scavenger
hunts, imaginative worlds, drawing and making, and hands-on collections. An only
child doesn't need a playmate to stay absorbed. They need a specific thing to do that
belongs entirely to them, so open time turns into an adventure instead of a stall.

"There's no one to play with." If you're raising an only child, that sentence has
probably found you at the exact moment you sat down. When there's no sibling to send
them off with, the screen starts looking like the world's most convenient playmate:
always available, never argues, never gets tired. If you've handed it over more times
than you'd like, you're not failing. You're outnumbered by a device that never needs
a nap.

But here's the reframe worth holding onto: **an only child isn't missing a playmate.
They're holding something most kids rarely get, whole stretches of time that belong
entirely to them.** No sibling grabbing the good pieces, no negotiating whose turn it
is, no game getting vetoed halfway through. The trick isn't finding your child
company. It's helping them discover that they *are* good company, and that solo play
can be just as absorbing as any screen.

## Give them a mission, not a playmate

Here's the thing about "go play by yourself": it's vague, and vague is where kids
stall. An open afternoon feels like an empty room. A *mission* feels like an
adventure that's already started.

A solo mission has three ingredients: a clear goal, a little challenge, and total
ownership. "Find five things in the yard smaller than your thumb." "Deliver this
note to the mailbox without being spotted." "Build something taller than the dog."
Notice that none of these need a second kid. In fact, they're often *better* alone,
because your child gets to be the hero, the planner, and the judge all at once.

## Personal records: a rival who never goes home

An only child doesn't have a built-in competitor, but they have the best one
available anywhere: yesterday's version of themselves. Beating your own record is a
game with no arguments, no sore losers, and no end.

Start a "house records" notebook. How far can they jump? How long can they balance
on one foot? How many times can they bounce a ball without missing? Write the record
down, date it, and watch what happens: that number becomes a quiet magnet. Kids will
head outside on their own, unprompted, just to take another crack at it.

[The Long Jump Record](/app/quest/the-long-jump-record) is a perfect first entry.
Mark a starting line, jump, mark the landing, then spend a happy half hour trying to
out-jump the mark. It sounds simple. That's the point. Simple games with a number
attached have a way of eating an entire afternoon.

## Collections and the one-kid museum

Collecting might be the single most only-child-friendly hobby there is. It's
self-paced, it's personal, and it grows a little every day. Interesting rocks,
pressed leaves, bottle caps, feathers, "possibly ancient" bits of pottery from the
garden. The collection itself is only half of it. The other half is *curating*:
sorting, labeling, arranging the display shelf, deciding what makes the cut.

Then comes the best part: the museum tour. Your child walks you through the
exhibit after dinner, explains each piece, and answers questions from the audience
(you, holding your coffee, asking excellent questions). They get your full attention
as the expert, which is a very different feeling from needing you as the
entertainment.

It's a collection, an art project, and an exhibition rolled into one kid-sized

## Worlds with a population of one

Nobody builds imaginary worlds like an only child, because nobody's there to say
"that's not how the game works." A patch of the backyard becomes a kingdom. The
space under the stairs becomes a headquarters. Maps get drawn, flags get designed,
laws get written (usually involving dessert).

Feed this instead of filling it. Offer paper for maps, a box of "supplies," and a
respectful knock before entering their territory. A world of their own is the
deepest kind of solo play there is, the kind where they forget to check what time
it is.
