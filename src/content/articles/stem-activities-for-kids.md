Ask ten people what STEM looks like and most will picture a screen: an app with a
coding robot, a math game with badges, a worksheet with a rocket in the corner. All
fine. None of it is the real thing.

Real STEM is a kid crouched over a puddle, jabbing a stick in to see how deep it goes.
It is a block tower that fell for the third time and the small engineer starting
to figure out why. **Science, technology, engineering, and math are not subjects you
download. They are things a kid does with their hands, outside, usually a little
muddy.** Better still, you do not need a kit, a lab, or a single dollar of gear to
start. You need a backyard and a good question.

## Build it, test it, break it, build it better

Engineering is the easiest one to start, because failing is built right in. Hand your
kid blocks, cardboard, sticks, or tape and give them a job with a clear
pass-or-fail line: a bridge that holds a can of beans, or a ramp that launches a toy
car as far as it will go.

Then the real teaching happens, because the first version fails. The bridge sags, the
car nose-dives off the end. Resist the urge to fix it for them. Ask "why do you think
it fell?" and let them test one change at a time: a wider base, thicker supports, a
gentler slope. That loop of build, test, fail, adjust is the whole heart of
engineering, and a kid can run it a dozen times before lunch.

- **Make them predict first.** Before each test, have them call the result out loud. A
  guess they have to defend turns a pile of blocks into an experiment.
- **Measure the win.** Stretch a tape measure to where the car stopped and write the
  number down. Beating your own record is math with a payoff.
- **Sneak in some tech.** A lever from a ruler and a pencil, a pulley from a spool and
  string: simple machines are the original technology, and building one beats reading
  about it.

## Turn a puddle into a lab

After it rains, there is a free science station in your driveway. Give your kid
sidewalk chalk and have them trace the exact edge of a puddle. Now ask the
question that makes it science: where will that edge be in an hour?

They predict, you both wander off, and come back to measure how far the
water crept in. That is evaporation, measurement, and prediction rolled into something
that looks like play. Trace one puddle in the sun and one in the shade, and let your
kid work out why they shrink at different speeds. If inventing that on the spot
sounds like work, [Puddle Scientist](/app/quest/puddle-scientist) lays it out as a
quest your kid can run start to finish.

## Hunt for the small stuff

Biology does not need a microscope. It needs a kid willing to look closely. Send yours
out to find and count every different bug they can, then sort the haul: how many legs,
how many wings, which hide under rocks and which bask on leaves.

Sorting living things by their traits is classification, the exact move a biologist
makes, and to your kid it is a treasure hunt that crawls. Keep asking the follow-up
question. "Why do you think the pill bug curled up when you touched it?" A reasoned
wrong guess beats a handed-over right answer every time. [The Bug Safari](/app/quest/the-bug-safari)
turns the whole thing into a ready-made mission, and you do not need to know a single
bug name to run it.

## Predict the weather, then check your work

Meteorology is mostly just paying attention, and it might be the most satisfying STEM
of all, because the world grades the homework for you. Have your kid study the sky
tonight, notice the clouds and which way the wind pushes them, and commit to a
call: rain tomorrow, or not?

Write the prediction down. Check it in the morning. A prediction you actually test is
science stripped to its purest form, and being wrong is not a flop, it is data for the
next guess. Watching a storm roll in from a safe window is that same skill turned up
loud. [Storm Watcher](/app/quest/storm-watcher) gives your kid a real reason to read
the sky instead of a screen.
