Week three of summer break has a very specific feeling. Camp is over, the novelty of
sleeping in has worn off, the pool has been "done," and "I'm bored" now starts before
you've finished your coffee. By 9am. Every day. If the tablet has been quietly
absorbing more and more of those hours, that's not a failure. That's just what
happens when a kid has six empty weeks and a screen is the closest thing to grab.

The fix isn't a packed schedule or a heroic Pinterest summer. It's a small stack of
ready-to-go boredom busters, so that when the whining starts, you have an answer that
isn't "go watch something." Here's what actually works for the long middle of summer.

## Give every day one mission

A whole empty summer day is overwhelming, for you *and* for your kid. But one
specific mission? That's doable. Here's the reframe that saves the season: **kids
don't need a full schedule. They need one good mission a day.**

A mission is not "go play outside" (too vague, instantly refused). It's specific and
a little bit silly: build a bug hotel out of sticks, find five things in the yard
smaller than your thumb, make a paper boat and race it down the gutter after the
sprinkler runs. Specific beats open-ended every single time, because a mission is a
puzzle and "go play" is a chore.

## Turn the backyard into a challenge arena

Kids will do almost anything if you attach a record to it. Not a prize, just a
number to beat. A few backyard classics:

- **The course.** Chalk arrows, spin circles, hop zones, a crawl-under-the-chair
  section. Time them, then let them redesign it to be harder.
  [Chalk Obstacle Course](/app/quest/chalk-obstacle-course) walks them through
  building one, and the best part is they'll run it twenty times trying to beat
  their own record.
- **The measurement wall.** Longest jump, tallest rock stack, farthest paper
  airplane. Write the records on the fence with chalk. A standing record is a
  standing invitation to come back tomorrow and break it.
- **The water round.** On the hot days, everything above works better soaked: run
  the course through the sprinkler, jump over the hose, race ice cubes down the
  slide.

The magic here is that a challenge arena refills itself. You set it up once, and
"I'm bored" becomes "I'm going to beat my time."

## Start a streak

Summer loses its shape without school, so give it a new one: a streak. One
outside-thing every day, tracked somewhere visible. A paper chain that grows a link
a day, X's on the fridge calendar, a jar that gets one marble per mission. Kids who
shrug at "go outside" will defend a fourteen-day streak with their lives, because
now it's not an activity, it's a *record*, and breaking it feels unthinkable.

## Save the golden hours for the good stuff

Midday summer heat is real, so don't fight it. Let the afternoon be quiet time,
reading, building, crafts, and spend the edges of the day outside instead. Mornings
are for missions and challenge courses, before the pavement gets hot.

You don't need all of this every day. Some days the win is one mission and a
popsicle. A summer where your kid built an obstacle course, kept a streak alive,
chased bugs through the yard, and watched a few sunsets is a genuinely great summer,
