Watch two kids share a single tablet and you'll notice something: they're sitting
shoulder to shoulder, but they aren't really together. One narrates, one grabs for
control, and the whole thing tips into a standoff over whose turn it is to hold it. A
screen built for one pair of hands doesn't teach cooperation. It teaches waiting, and a
low simmer of resentment.

Teamwork is a skill, and like any skill it grows with reps. Kids learn to cooperate by
actually cooperating: splitting up a job, bickering about it a little, working out who's
good at what, and landing on something they built together. **You don't have to teach
teamwork like a lesson. You just have to hand two kids a job big enough that they need
each other to finish it.**

Here are a few that pull kids together instead of apart, plus how to set them up so they
stay that way.

## Give them a build too big for one kid

Nothing turns two kids into a crew faster than a construction project, and a fort is the
classic for a reason. It needs a planner, a hauler, and an engineer, and no single kid
can be all three at once. Hand them blankets, chairs, and clothespins, add one rule (it
has to be big enough for both of them to sit inside), and step back.

The move is to let them split the roles themselves. When one kid takes "where does it go"
and the other takes "how do we keep the roof up," you've got a team instead of two kids
doing the same thing twice. [The Secret Fort](/app/quest/the-secret-fort) in Questling
lays this out as a quest with its own steps, so the build has a shared finish line and a
small payoff at the end, which is often the difference between "we started a fort" and
"we finished one."

Other builds work the same way: a marble run down the stairs, a pillow tower taller than
the shorter kid, a pretend shop with a counter and a sign. If it has parts, it has jobs,
and jobs are what turn two kids into a team.

## Make a course and race the clock, not each other

Relays and obstacle courses are teamwork in disguise, as long as you aim them right. The
usual mistake is setting up kid-versus-kid, which is lighter fluid on any rivalry. Flip
it so they're the team and the stopwatch is the opponent.

Draw a course across the driveway with chalk, hop through it, then try to beat your own
best time as a pair. [Chalk Obstacle Course](/app/quest/chalk-obstacle-course) is built
for exactly this. They design the course together (already a negotiation), then run it,
and "can we go faster next time" keeps them coming back. Because they built it, they both
defend it, and because they're racing the clock instead of each other, nobody has to be
the loser.

You can stretch it into a real relay with a baton, a stick or a wooden spoon works fine.
One runs the course, tags the other, and the clock only stops once both have gone. Now
they genuinely need each other to win, which is the entire point.

## Give them one goal neither can reach alone

The strongest teamwork comes from a mission where going solo simply doesn't work. Think
"the floor is lava": lay out cushions or sheets of paper as stepping stones, and the goal
is to get both kids across the room without touching the ground, moving the stones
forward as they go. One kid balances while the other passes stones back up the line. That
is cooperation you never have to lecture about, because the game collapses the second
they stop helping each other.

[The Stepping Stone Crossing](/app/quest/the-stepping-stone-crossing) is a ready-made
version, a shared crossing with a clear win only reached by working together. Other "need
each other" missions: a scavenger hunt with a list too long for one kid, a snack they
make as a two-person kitchen (one measures, one stirs), a short play they write and
perform for you after dinner.

If there's a big age gap, hand the older one a role here, captain, guide, or safety
officer, and the gap turns into something useful instead of a source of "she's doing it
wrong."
