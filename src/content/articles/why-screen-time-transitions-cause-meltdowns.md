You say "okay, time's up," and in half a second your calm, happy kid turns into
someone screaming on the floor like you just cancelled Christmas. It happens to
basically every family with a tablet in the house, and it can feel like your kid is
being dramatic on purpose. Usually they're not. Something real is going on, and
once you see it, the whole handoff gets a lot easier to manage.

## What's actually happening

Screens (especially games and videos) are built to hold attention and deliver
little hits of dopamine on a steady drip. Getting pulled off mid-drip is genuinely
uncomfortable for a developing brain that hasn't learned to self-regulate that
crash yet. On top of that, young kids are just bad at transitions in general,
screen or not. Ending a screen combines the hardest kind of stop (mid-stimulation)
with the hardest kind of skill (switching gears), so the big reaction isn't really
about defiance. It's closer to withdrawal plus a transition, stacked together.

## Warn before you end it

The single biggest fix is skipping the surprise stop. "Two more minutes, then
we're done" gives their brain time to downshift before the screen actually goes
off. A visible timer helps even more, since it turns "Dad just decided" into "the
timer decided," which is a much easier thing for a kid to accept.

## Let the content end it, not you

Where you can, let a natural stopping point do the work: the end of the episode,
the end of the level, the end of the song. "We stop after this song finishes" lands
completely differently than cutting it off mid-scene, even if the actual number of
minutes is nearly identical.

## Give them something to walk into

The hardest part of ending a screen is what comes after: nothing. An empty,
undefined stretch of time is its own small source of dread for a kid. Having the
next thing already lined up (lunch, a walk, a specific activity) turns "screen time
is over" into "screen time is over, and now we're doing the fun part," which is a
much easier trade to make.

None of this makes the meltdowns vanish overnight. But a warning, a natural
stopping point, and something good to walk into turn most of them from a full
production into a shrug and a "okay, fine." That's a fair trade for a few small
changes.
