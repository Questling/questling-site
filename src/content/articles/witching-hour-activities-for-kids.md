It has a name in a lot of houses: the witching hour. Somewhere around 4pm the whole
day seems to turn. You're trying to get dinner started, the pan is already hot, and
suddenly everyone within ten feet of you is falling apart. One kid is whining into
your leg, another is doing something you can hear but can't quite see, and the tablet
is sitting right there on the counter, quietly promising you twenty minutes of peace.

If that's your evening, you're not doing anything wrong, and neither are your kids.
**The witching hour isn't a behavior problem. It's a timing problem.** It's the exact
moment hunger, end-of-day tiredness, and a parent who has suddenly gone unavailable
all collide at once. Once you see it that way, the fix isn't a calmer kid. It's a
small set of activities that don't need you standing there running them.

## Build a fort and let them vanish into it

Few things buy a cooking parent more time than a good hideout. Drape a blanket over
the kitchen table, pull the couch cushions into a wall, hand over a flashlight, and
something shifts. A fort is a whole little world, and building it is half the fun
before they've even climbed inside.

The best part is that it runs itself. You're ten feet away at the stove, close enough
to hear everything, while they're busy under a blanket running their own show. Give it
a mission to make it stick: it's a spaceship, a bear cave, a secret base that needs a
password. In Questling, [The Secret Fort](/app/quest/the-secret-fort) turns the whole
thing into a quest with a goal, which is often the exact nudge a melting-down kid
needs to switch from crying to building.

## Set out a station that runs itself

The other reliable move is a table station you set up once and then walk away from.
The trick is picking something with almost no cleanup and no next-step questions, so
they're not back at your elbow ninety seconds later.

Painting rocks is close to perfect for this. A handful of smooth stones from the yard,
a few paints or markers, a sheet of newspaper, and a kid can disappear into it for a
good while. It's quiet, it's creative, and it keeps them at the table right where you
can glance over between stirs. [Rock Painting Gallery](/app/quest/rock-painting-gallery)
gives it a point beyond just painting: they're building a gallery, lining their
finished rocks along the windowsill or the front step for everyone to admire later.

## Send the big energy outside

A lot of the witching-hour meltdown is simply pent-up energy with nowhere to go. If
there's any daylight left and a driveway or backyard to use, outside is your best
reset. Movement burns off the crankiness in a way no indoor activity quite can.

A bucket of sidewalk chalk goes a surprisingly long way. Ask them to design a course
(hop through these squares, walk this wobbly line, spin around at the star) and then
actually run it. It's creative and physical at the same time, and best of all they can
do the whole thing without you. [Chalk Obstacle Course](/app/quest/chalk-obstacle-course)
hands them the challenge ready-made, so you can slip back inside and get the pasta on
while they wear themselves out in the fresh air.

## Or hand one of them a real job

Sometimes the kid clinging to your leg doesn't want to be sent off somewhere. They
want to be near you. So put them to work. Tear the lettuce, rinse the potatoes, stir
the cold pot, set the table, count out the forks. A real job, not busywork they can
tell is fake, turns a whiny helper into a proud one.

Yes, dinner takes a few minutes longer this way. But a five-year-old who is convinced
they're making dinner is a five-year-old who isn't melting down, and that trade is
almost always worth it.
